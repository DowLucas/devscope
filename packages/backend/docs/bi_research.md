# What every layer of leadership wants from engineering analytics

**The demand for engineering intelligence is surging — Gartner predicts 50% of software engineering organizations will adopt Software Engineering Intelligence (SEI) platforms by 2027, up from just 5% in 2024.** This tenfold growth is driven by three converging forces: CEOs demanding the same visibility into engineering that they have into sales and marketing, the explosion of AI coding tools requiring ROI justification, and the McKinsey-fueled realization that engineering leaders who don't measure proactively risk having worse frameworks imposed on them. DevScope AI Intelligence sits at the center of this wave — but winning requires a precise understanding of what each buyer persona actually wants, how they want it delivered, and what the competition is doing wrong.

This report synthesizes research across industry analysts (Gartner, Forrester), product review sites (G2, Capterra), competitive product pages, engineering leadership blogs (Will Larson, Charity Majors, Gergely Orosz), and market benchmarking data to provide persona-specific guidance across metrics, delivery preferences, proactive insights, competitive positioning, sales strategy, and product roadmap.

---

## Engineering managers want operational clarity, not more dashboards

Engineering managers and tech leads sit at the most operationally demanding layer. They check metrics near-daily, juggle 6+ fragmented tools, and manually assemble weekly reports. Their core frustration is captured by one EM who noted: "Wrong stack means you're spending three hours a day in status update meetings that could've been a dashboard check."

**The metrics that matter most to EMs form a clear hierarchy.** At the foundation sit the four DORA metrics — deployment frequency, lead time for changes, change failure rate, and mean time to recovery — which every source identifies as baseline expectations. Above that, EMs track daily operational flow: cycle time (the single most-cited metric for daily management), PR review time (to spot review bottlenecks), sprint velocity, and code churn. The third tier is resource allocation — what percentage of time goes to new features versus maintenance versus tech debt. This metric is what EMs use to negotiate for roadmap time with leadership. Finally, EMs increasingly track qualitative signals: developer satisfaction, burnout risk indicators, and onboarding ramp-up time, reflecting the SPACE framework's emphasis on the human dimension.

**The questions EMs ask daily reveal what "actionable" means at this level.** They want to know: Who is blocked right now? Is any engineer stuck on the same PR for three or more days? Who is carrying too much code review load? Are we on track for this sprint? Where are the bottlenecks — code review, testing, or deployment? Weekly, they ask: Where does engineering time actually go? Is anyone at risk of burnout? How is our delivery predictability trending? These questions drive specific decisions — reassigning work, prioritizing tech debt sprints, changing code review processes, advocating for tooling budgets, and justifying headcount requests.

**EMs want data delivered via Slack alerts for real-time issues** (stale PRs, working agreement violations), weekly email digests summarizing trends, and dashboards that replace status meetings — not add to them. They strongly prefer trend lines over point-in-time snapshots, benchmarks against their own historical data rather than external comparisons, and drill-down capability from organization to team to individual diagnostic views. The ideal anomaly detection for an EM includes PR velocity drops, cycle time regressions over two-plus sprints, review bottleneck formation, deployment failure rate spikes, and sprint scope creep. Critically, alerts must include context and root cause analysis, not just threshold notifications, to avoid alert fatigue.

Will Larson, CTO of Carta, frames this precisely: "The number one measurement risk is measuring nothing because you're trying to measure everything." He categorizes EM metrics into four modes — planning, operating, optimizing, and inspiring — and warns against mixing them. DevScope's conversational analytics could let EMs ask natural language questions ("Which team has the longest PR review time this sprint?") rather than building elaborate spreadsheet workarounds, which multiple sources cite as the current reality.

## CTOs and VPs need board-ready translation and capacity forecasting

CTOs and VPs of Engineering operate at the strategic layer where engineering work must be justified in business terms. Their defining challenge was captured in a VentureBeat analysis: "At startups, it's often the CEO telling the CTO they want to see some metrics, as they're concerned how much the engineering team costs and want to promote efficiency." The CTO's fear is being evaluated as a cost center; their ambition is being recognized as a value creator.

**The strategic metrics CTOs prioritize extend well beyond DORA.** While DORA remains foundational, CTOs layer on investment allocation tracking (the split between new features, keep-the-lights-on work, and technical debt — the single metric boards care most about), R&D spend as a percentage of revenue, cost per feature, and engineering ROI. Developers spend an estimated **23–33% of their time dealing with technical debt** according to Deloitte research, and high-performing organizations allocate 20–30% of capacity to debt reduction. CTOs need this data visible and trending over time.

The ROI measurement challenge is acute. As one CTO advisor noted: "When asked 'What is Engineering's ROI?' the CTO feels pressured to answer it, but often doesn't have the tools. So the CTO counters with DORA metrics, uptime percentages, and developer experience evaluations. None of these translate into a bottom line revenue number." The most effective frameworks follow an Effort → Output → Outcome → Impact hierarchy (articulated by Gergely Orosz and Kent Beck), where DORA and SPACE metrics sit at the outcome/impact end — which is where measurement should focus. One CTO shared an example where a focused effort to reduce churn cut monthly customer churn in half, increasing projected annual revenue by millions on a relatively small engineering investment. That causally connected story is the kind of ROI narrative that resonates.

**CTOs want reporting in scorecard format with trend indicators** (improving, stable, declining) for executive and CEO audiences, detailed team-level dashboards for their own weekly reviews, and quarterly board decks combining narrative with three to four key metrics. The recommended board presentation structure includes recent successes mapped to business value, investment allocation trends, engineering health metrics, security posture, and a forward-looking roadmap with milestones. One company replaced static slide decks with a live analytics dashboard in leadership syncs, showing engineering initiatives progressing week by week — a format that resonated far more than quarterly retrospectives.

**Proactive intelligence for CTOs centers on delivery risk and capacity.** They want delivery risk forecasting (will this initiative ship on time?), bottleneck detection across teams, capacity forecasting accounting for PTO and historical velocity, technical debt trajectory prediction, attrition risk signals, and increasingly, AI tool impact measurement. Benchmarking is critical at this level — CTOs want to compare teams against each other on common metrics while also benchmarking against industry tiers (DORA's elite/high/medium/low classification). LinearB's 2025 Engineering Benchmarks Report, analyzing 3,000+ teams, and DX's Core 4 industry benchmarks are the reference datasets CTOs already use.

## CEOs want engineering to look like every other department: measurable

The CEO perspective is defined by one fundamental frustration: engineering is a black box. As one analysis put it: "Market needs and requirements went in the front. Something magical and unknown happened in the middle. Product designs popped out the back." Sales has pipeline projections, HR has people analytics, marketing has attribution models — engineering has nothing comparable for most organizations.

**CEOs ask five core business questions about engineering.** First: "You have so many engineers — why are you not shipping more features?" This is the number one frustration cited across sources. Second: "When will this new functionality be ready?" Third: "How much does it cost to support each product line?" Fourth: "What is at risk right now?" Fifth: "Is engineering improving over time?" These are not technical questions. They are business questions about velocity, predictability, cost, risk, and trajectory — and they require translation, not raw metrics.

**The translation gap is the core problem DevScope can solve for this persona.** Traditional engineering metrics don't resonate with executives. As Harness SEI research notes: "Story points, commit counts, and deployment logs mean little to a CFO, CMO, or CEO. To gain influence, engineering leaders need to frame their work in business terms — think predictability, customer impact, cost efficiency, and revenue acceleration." The business-level metrics CEOs actually understand include timeline adherence measured over four-plus quarters, cost per product line, investment allocation percentages, revenue per engineer, R&D as a percentage of revenue, and production risk scorecards using traffic-light indicators across product lines.

**CEO dashboard preferences are radically minimalist.** "A good executive dashboard has 5-7 KPIs. A great one has 3-4." Exception-based design is paramount — executives don't need to review metrics that are on track; dashboards should highlight only what's off-track. Format must be visual (gauge charts, trend lines, traffic lights), narrative-driven ("what happened, why it matters, what we're doing about it"), and always show actual versus target with variance. The frequency preference is monthly executive summaries with quarterly deep-dives for the board. Real-time data is explicitly unnecessary at this level — as one design principle states: "Executives don't need real-time data. Daily or weekly is usually sufficient."

CEOs make five categories of decisions with engineering data: **hiring and resource allocation** (where to add engineers), **budget distribution** across product lines and tech debt, **project prioritization** (which initiatives deliver the most business value), **timeline commitments** to customers and the market, and increasingly, **AI tool investment decisions** (the copilot bill, as Forrester puts it, has come due). R&D capitalization is a growing demand area — US companies have capitalized approximately 17% of engineering expenditures, and post-2022 IRS rules require all R&D costs to be capitalized for tax purposes, creating direct demand for granular engineering activity tracking.

## The competitive landscape reveals a clear gap in conversational intelligence

The developer productivity tools market includes roughly ten significant players, each targeting different personas with varying levels of AI sophistication. The analysis reveals a critical whitespace opportunity for DevScope.

**Jellyfish** is the enterprise incumbent, targeting VPs and CTOs with patented allocation modeling, capacity planning, and DevFinOps (R&D capitalization). It serves 700+ customers and 11,000+ engineering teams but is perceived as a management tool, not a developer tool — creating trust friction. **LinearB** leads on workflow automation with its gitStream policy-as-code PR routing and is pivoting aggressively toward AI (AI code reviews, MCP server integration, GenAI impact measurement), targeting EMs and DevEx leads at roughly $16M ARR with 45% year-over-year growth. **DX** (formerly GetDX), founded by the creators of DORA and SPACE, dominates developer experience measurement through its research-backed DXI score and Core 4 framework, serving 800+ organizations including Dropbox, Booking.com, and Pfizer.

**Swarmia** positions as developer-friendly with working agreements and transparent team-level metrics, while **Faros AI** takes the most flexible approach with an open data model and the most ambitious conversational AI feature (Lighthouse). **Allstacks** leads on predictive analytics with ML-powered delivery forecasting and agentic AI that creates tickets and assigns follow-ups. **Sleuth** has the most accurate DORA implementation (A+ rated) but is narrowly focused on deployment tracking. **Pluralsight Flow** (originally GitPrime) was acquired by Appfire in February 2025, leaving its future uncertain, and it carries legacy perception as a surveillance tool.

**The most revealing finding is what's missing across all competitors.** No tool delivers true conversational engineering intelligence — the ability to ask natural language questions and receive contextual, prescriptive answers from engineering data. Faros AI has basic conversational chat but limited adoption evidence. No tool effectively serves all three personas (EM, CTO, CEO) from a single platform; buyers must choose between executive dashboards (Jellyfish), developer tools (Sleuth/Haystack), or DevEx measurement (DX). Most "AI features" across competitors remain surface-level — GPT wrappers generating PR descriptions and metric summaries rather than genuine diagnostic and predictive intelligence. And no tool closes the insight-to-action loop: they identify problems but don't recommend or orchestrate solutions.

Common user complaints from G2 and Gartner Peer Insights cluster around five themes: surveillance perception (the universal adoption killer), data accuracy discrepancies versus raw source exports, metrics without actionability ("lots of data but unclear what to do"), pricing friction as teams scale, and setup complexity requiring weeks of configuration before value appears.

## Selling requires separate playbooks for builders and buyers

The sales motion for engineering analytics is uniquely challenging because the buyer and the end user are often different people with opposing concerns. The EM or CTO evaluates business value; the developers whose data feeds the system evaluate whether it feels like surveillance. **66% of developers don't believe current productivity metrics reflect their contributions** (DX Research 2025), and 37% report weekly privacy harm experiences from measurement tools. This trust deficit is the single largest adoption barrier.

**Messaging must be persona-specific.** For engineering managers, the resonant message is: "Remove bottlenecks so your team ships faster without burnout." Lead with team-level system insights, not individual tracking. Swarmia's positioning is exemplary: "Systematically eliminating anything that gets in the way of delivery." For CTOs, the message shifts to: "Translate engineering work into business outcomes the board understands." The critical pain point, articulated by Gergely Orosz, is that "if engineering leaders try to avoid the question of how to measure developer productivity, they risk the CEO or CFO turning to McKinsey." For CEOs, the message is simplest and most powerful: "Finally see engineering as clearly as you see sales and marketing."

**Five common objections require rehearsed responses.** The surveillance objection ("this is Big Brother for developers") is handled by defaulting to team-level metrics only, making the same dashboards visible to developers and managers, and explicitly positioning against individual ranking. The gaming objection ("developers will just split PRs and rubber-stamp reviews") is addressed by using balanced, oppositional metrics — DX Core 4 pairs speed versus effectiveness so gaming one dimension hurts another. The tool fatigue objection ("we already have dashboards everywhere") is countered by positioning as consolidation — a single source of truth replacing fragmented, conflicting tools. The philosophical objection ("you can't measure creative work") is reframed: "We measure the conditions for great work, not the work itself." And the culture damage objection ("we tried metrics before and it hurt us") requires explicitly acknowledging the harm of McKinsey-style approaches and leading with developer experience measurement first.

**ROI narratives that close deals follow four patterns.** Time savings: DX's DXI metric shows each point gained saves 13 minutes per developer weekly — for 500 engineers, that's 108 hours per week reclaimed. AI validation: METR research found developers using AI tools are actually **19% slower despite feeling 20% faster**, and LinearB's 2026 benchmarks show AI-generated PRs have only a 32.7% acceptance rate versus 84.4% for manual PRs — creating urgent demand for objective AI impact measurement. Retention: replacing a senior engineer costs $150K–$300K, so reducing attrition by even 5% through better developer experience measurement pays for the tool many times over. And delivery speed: organizations with top-quartile DevEx scores show **4–5x higher engineering speed and quality** than bottom-quartile performers.

## The market is heading toward AI-native, framework-unified platforms

Three forces are reshaping the product roadmap for engineering intelligence tools: the convergence of measurement frameworks, the shift from dashboards to agentic AI, and the explosion of platform engineering metrics.

**Framework convergence is happening rapidly.** DORA (now expanded from four to five metrics with the addition of Rework Rate), SPACE, and DevEx are merging into unified approaches. DX launched Core 4 in January 2025 with four dimensions — Speed, Effectiveness, Quality, and Impact — claiming to unify all prior frameworks. GitHub released its Engineering System Success Playbook (ESSP) with a three-zone model. The DORA 2025 Report replaced its low-to-elite tier system with seven team archetypes and introduced an AI Capabilities Model with seven foundational practices. The industry consensus: no single metric captures productivity. Tools that automatically select and weight the right metrics for each team's maturity level — rather than forcing teams to choose frameworks — will win.

**AI capabilities are evolving from summaries to agents.** Current "AI features" across competitors are mostly descriptive (what happened) or surface-level (generated PR descriptions, metric summaries). The frontier is prescriptive and agentic: Allstacks deploys AI agents that detect delivery risks, explain root causes, and create tickets with proposed owners. Gartner predicts 80% of developer productivity insight platforms will include workflow automation by 2028. The ultimate gap is the insight-to-action loop — going beyond "cycle time is high" to automatically routing improvement actions with human approval.

**AI copilot ROI measurement is the hottest emerging category.** With 85% of developers now using AI coding tools, every platform is racing to add impact tracking. But the measurement challenge is nuanced — adoption rates alone don't prove value. Organizations need to measure acceptance rates, code quality comparison (AI versus manual), developer satisfaction changes, and whether AI-generated code accumulates technical debt faster. Faros AI calls this their most popular intelligence module. DX added a full AI Measurement product covering strategic planning, vendor evaluation, usage analytics, and impact analysis. DevScope should treat AI impact intelligence as a core module, not an add-on.

**Critical integrations follow a clear priority order.** GitHub plus Jira plus Slack covers approximately 80% of the data needed on day one. Adding GitLab, Azure DevOps, and CI/CD tools (GitHub Actions, Jenkins, CircleCI) reaches 95% of enterprise scenarios. Incident management tools (PagerDuty, OpsGenie) are essential for MTTR metrics. Calendar integrations enable maker-time analysis. And emerging integrations with IDE telemetry (VS Code, JetBrains) and internal developer portals (Backstage, with 89% market share among IDP adopters) represent differentiators for forward-looking teams. The setup promise should be: "Value in the first week, not the first quarter."

## Conclusion: three strategic imperatives for DevScope AI Intelligence

The research reveals a market that is growing explosively but remains fragmented and immature. **The conversational AI interface is DevScope's most differentiated capability** — no competitor has delivered a genuinely useful "talk to your engineering data" experience, and Gartner specifically highlights natural language interfaces as the emerging frontier. This should be the hero feature in every demo.

**The three-persona strategy must be deliberate, not accidental.** Build for EMs first (they drive daily adoption and developer trust), package for CTOs second (they write the check and need board-ready reporting), and translate for CEOs third (they approve the budget and need three to four business-language KPIs, not forty engineering metrics). The tool that serves all three from a single data model — with role-appropriate views — fills the most significant competitive gap.

**Developer trust is not a feature; it is the product.** Every successful tool in this space has learned that if engineers feel surveilled, they resist — and resistance is fatal for data quality. Default to team-level metrics. Make individual data visible only to the individual. Frame everything as process improvement, not performance judgment. Will Larson's warning should be a design principle: "As long as tooling keeps privileging the manager who wants to grade their team rather than learn from the development process, these tools will be actively distrusted by the engineers who instrument them." DevScope's AI-powered insights should surface system problems, not people problems — and when the data does point to individual patterns, the tool should route that insight to the individual privately, not to their manager.
